# What volunteer work can buy you that a job posting can't

Every hiring cycle produces the same complaint from newer engineers: there are no entry-level jobs, or the ones that exist all want experience nobody will give you the chance to earn. The complaint is understandable and mostly beside the point, because there's a route around it that most people overlook simply because it doesn't look like a job search. I've used it repeatedly, across music, writing, and cloud engineering, and it has worked every time I've tried it: volunteer for the work you actually want to be doing.

This isn't a consolation prize for people who can't land a paid role. It's a deliberate trade: you give real, visible effort before anyone is contractually obligated to value it, and in exchange you get to compress years of learning into months, build relationships that outlast the project, and audition for the job before anyone has to take a risk on hiring you.

## Volunteering compresses the learning curve

The first thing volunteering buys you is speed. Deploying cloud infrastructure for free on a real project taught me more in a few months than any course could have, because the stakes were real even though the pay wasn't. The same was true writing for a travel publication on my own dime: I learned to tell a story under a deadline far faster than I would have freelancing my way in cautiously. Volunteer work tends to come with fewer gatekeepers too. Full-time staff are often more open to explaining how something works to a volunteer than they would be to a competing hire, because there's no competitive tension in the relationship yet.

## It converts to paid work more often than people expect

The second thing it buys you is a direct path to being hired, and in my experience this isn't the exception, it's close to the default outcome. I once walked into a travel agency and told the owner plainly: I'll work for free, just teach me. Three months later, I was on payroll. The same pattern played out in cloud work: a volunteer project turned into a full-time engineering role, because by the time the company needed to hire, they'd already seen exactly how I worked.

That's the part a job application can't replicate. A resume is a claim. A finished volunteer project is evidence.

## The relationships outlast the project

Every volunteer effort I've taken on has left me with people I can call later, some of whom have reached back out themselves, even without an open role, just to say they'd bring me in if one appeared. Networking built this way doesn't feel transactional, because it wasn't set up to be. It's simply what happens when you work alongside people on something real.

## The skills that grow are broader than the ones you're there for

Running a volunteer effort, especially one with no manager checking in, forces you to develop judgment you don't get handed a manual for: how to prioritize, how to lead a small group of people who also aren't being paid, how to solve problems without anyone above you to escalate to. That combination, technical skill plus the leadership it takes to run something with no one watching, is exactly what shows up later as "ready for more responsibility" on a resume. I moved from newcomer to lead engineer largely because I'd already demonstrated, unpaid, that I could carry that weight.

## The only rule that matters: bring value, not just time

None of this works if you show up and do the minimum. The trick isn't volunteering, it's what you do once you're there. I never told anyone I'd simply help out. I told them I'd add real value, and then I made sure that was true. Code that's actually good. Work that's actually finished. That's the difference between volunteering and being tolerated.

I hold myself to a rule because of this: I'm never without an opportunity for more than three months, not because I'm exceptional, but because I always have somewhere I can offer real effort while I look for the next paid role. Idle time is a choice, and it's one I've learned not to make.

## Judgment, applied to your own career

Most career advice treats volunteering as a stopgap, something you do while waiting for a real opportunity. I'd argue it's closer to the opposite: it's one of the few ways to apply judgment directly to your own career, choosing exactly where to invest unpaid effort so it compounds into something real, rather than waiting for someone else to decide you're ready. If you're early in your career and stuck on the experience paradox, the fastest way through it usually isn't a better resume. It's finding one real project, showing up, and giving it more than anyone asked for.
